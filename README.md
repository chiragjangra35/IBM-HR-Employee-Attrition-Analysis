üìù **Executive Summary**
This project focuses on HR Analytics using the IBM HR Attrition dataset. The goal was to identify factors contributing to employee turnover and provide data-driven recommendations to improve retention.

The analysis reveals that Income, Age/Marital Status, and Job Role are the primary drivers of attrition. Specifically, employees with lower monthly incomes, single employees, and those in Sales roles face the highest risk of leaving.


üíº **Business Problem**
Retaining top talent is a critical challenge for any organization. High attrition rates result in:

Increased recruitment and training costs.

Loss of productivity and institutional knowledge.

Lower employee morale.

Objective: To design a dashboard that helps the HR team monitor attrition rates, identify "flight risk" profiles, and take proactive measures to retain talent.

**1. Overview of Attrition**

**2. Salary & Income Analysis**
The Income Gap: There is a distinct correlation between low income and high attrition. The "Monthly Income Distribution" chart shows that employees who left (Orange Line) consistently earned below the average compared to those who stayed (Pink Line).

Key Finding: Attrition is highest in the lower salary slabs (below 5k/month).

**3. Demographic Factors**
Marital Status: "Single" employees have a significantly higher attrition rate (25.5%) compared to Married (12.5%) or Divorced (10.1%) employees.

Age Group: Younger employees are more likely to leave, indicating a need for better early-career engagement.

**4. Job Role & Tenure**
High-Risk Roles: Sales Representatives, Laboratory Technicians, and Human Resources roles show the highest turnover percentages.

The "New Manager" Effect: There is a spike in attrition for employees with 0 years under a current manager, suggesting onboarding friction or management mismatch.
